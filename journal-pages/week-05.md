# Week 05 - Reflective Proposal

[← Back to Home](../index.md)

The experiment I found most engaging was the Interactive Data Portrait, as it shifted my focus from abstract or large‑scale datasets to personal, everyday data. Working with my own data made the process more meaningful and reflective, highlighting how interaction can transform data from static information into a participatory experience. This experience reinforced my interest in non‑traditional data sources such as personal, behavioural, and lived experience data, and aligns with course discussions around data humanism and the datafication of everyday life, where data is understood as subjective and shaped by human experience rather than purely objective.

Building on these ideas, this project that I plan to do explores the everyday experience of how time is spent versus how it is perceived. Many people feel constantly busy, yet their understanding of their own time use is influenced by memory, emotion, and cultural expectations around productivity. The project examines the gap between perspective and reality by using personal time‑use data as both the subject and material of the work.

The data will be self‑collected through short periods of manual time tracking and translated into a physical form using yarn. Different colours of yarn will represent categories such as productivity (work and study), rest (sleeping and eating), social time, or distraction, and the position on the board will represent if the task felt long or short. 

![Visual Example of Data Display Idea](<../assets/week-05/Visual Idea.jpeg>)
*Visual Example of Data Display Idea*

Yarn is deliberately imprecise and tactile, allowing time to be represented as messy, embodied, and subjective. This method reflects how time is actually experienced and makes visible the interpretive labour involved in data collection. By comparing the yarn‑based mapping with my perceived experience of the day, the project highlights slippages between how time feels and how it is recorded.

The resulting visualisation will take the form of a physical data portrait, displayed on a board with pins or nails. The yarn is likely to overlap, knot, twist, loop, vary in tension, length or trail unevenly, visually reflecting the fragmented and non‑linear nature of daily life. The work functions as a slow, analogue alternative to automated self‑tracking systems and may include simple interactive elements, such as inviting viewers to guess how time was spent or encouraging them to imagine how their own day might look if mapped in the same way. Rather than aiming for accuracy, the visualisation foregrounds subjectivity, uncertainty, and interpretation.

The central provocation questions how reliable our perceptions of busyness and productivity are, and how much trust we place in data to explain our lives. By contrasting assumed time use with the messy reality of the yarn mapping, the project asks what counts as productive time and what is overlooked or flattened by data systems. For a public audience, the intended impact is a shift in perspective—from viewing time as something to optimise, to recognising it as uneven, lived, and deeply human. As a proposal, the project remains open to iterative development, with potential future directions including multiple days of data, audience participation, or comparisons between manual and automated representations.
